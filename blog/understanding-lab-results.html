Understanding Your Lab Results

Published January 5, 2026 • 9 min read

You get your lab results back, and everything is marked "normal"—but you still don't feel well. Or perhaps something's flagged as high or low, but your doctor says "let's just watch it." What's actually going on?

Understanding lab results requires looking beyond the reference ranges to see what your numbers reveal about your health from multiple perspectives.

The "Normal" Range Problem

Laboratory reference ranges are typically based on statistical averages from the population tested at that lab. Being "within range" doesn't necessarily mean optimal—it means you're within 2 standard deviations of the population average.

Three issues with standard ranges:

  • They're population-based, not individual-based: Your optimal values may differ from statistical averages
  • They're wide: Being on the low end of "normal" thyroid function is very different from being optimal
  • They miss functional ranges: Functional medicine uses tighter ranges that better predict health outcomes

Key Lab Tests Explained

Complete Blood Count (CBC)

What it shows: Red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets, hemoglobin, hematocrit

Western interpretation: Screens for anemia, infection, immune system disorders, clotting issues

Functional medicine view: Looks for subclinical patterns. Hemoglobin at the low end of normal plus low ferritin may indicate iron insufficiency even if not technically anemic. High normal white blood cells could suggest inflammation.

TCM correlation: Low hemoglobin/RBC relates to Blood deficiency. High WBC may indicate Heat or Wind invasion.

Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP)

What it shows: Blood sugar, kidney function, electrolytes, liver enzymes

Blood Glucose:

  • Standard range: 70-99 mg/dL fasting
  • Functional range: 75-85 mg/dL optimal
  • What high-normal means: Fasting glucose of 95-99 suggests insulin resistance developing even though "normal"

Liver Enzymes (ALT, AST):

  • High values: Liver stress, fatty liver, inflammation
  • Pattern matters: AST higher than ALT suggests different issues than ALT dominant
  • Functional view: Even mildly elevated enzymes warrant investigation into diet, toxin exposure, metabolic health

Lipid Panel

Beyond total cholesterol: The ratios and particle sizes matter more than total numbers.

Key markers:

  • HDL: Higher is better (60+ is protective)
  • Triglycerides: Below 100 is optimal (standard says below 150)
  • HDL/Triglyceride ratio: Should be under 2 (divide triglycerides by HDL)
  • LDL particle size: Small, dense LDL particles are more problematic than large, fluffy ones (requires advanced testing)

What high triglycerides really mean: Often indicates metabolic dysfunction, insulin resistance, and high carbohydrate intake—not just "high fat diet" as commonly assumed.

Thyroid Panel

Standard test (TSH only): Misses most thyroid dysfunction

Comprehensive panel includes:

  • TSH: Pituitary signal to thyroid
  • Free T4: Inactive thyroid hormone
  • Free T3: Active thyroid hormone (most important)
  • Reverse T3: Inactive form that blocks T3
  • Thyroid antibodies: TPO and Thyroglobulin (detects autoimmune thyroid disease)

Common pattern: TSH in "normal" range but low Free T3 and high Reverse T3—indicates conversion problem. Causes include stress, inflammation, nutrient deficiencies (selenium, zinc, iron).

Functional ranges:

  • TSH: 1.0-2.0 (not 0.5-5.0)
  • Free T3: Upper half of range
  • Free T4: Mid-range
  • Reverse T3: Under 15

Vitamin D

Standard range: 30-100 ng/mL

Functional range: 50-70 ng/mL

Optimal for immune function: 60-80 ng/mL

Why it matters: Vitamin D isn't just for bones. It's crucial for immune function, mood, hormone balance, and inflammation control. Most people are deficient (under 30) or insufficient (30-50).

Iron Panel

Don't just check hemoglobin: Full iron panel includes:

  • Serum iron: Current circulating iron
  • Ferritin: Iron storage (most important)
  • TIBC: Total iron binding capacity
  • Transferrin saturation: Percentage of iron-carrying capacity being used

Low ferritin (under 50): Even if hemoglobin is "normal," low ferritin causes fatigue, hair loss, weakness, and impairs thyroid function. Optimal ferritin for women: 70-100.

Reading Between the Lines

Pattern Recognition

Individual markers tell part of the story. Patterns reveal more:

  • High triglycerides + low HDL + high-normal glucose: Metabolic syndrome developing
  • High-normal TSH + low ferritin + low B12: Nutrient deficiencies affecting thyroid conversion
  • Elevated liver enzymes + high CRP + high ferritin: Inflammatory process affecting multiple systems

Trend Analysis

One test is a snapshot. Multiple tests over time show trends:

  • Fasting glucose creeping up over years? Early insulin resistance
  • Ferritin dropping despite supplementation? Absorption issues or ongoing loss
  • TSH gradually rising? Thyroid beginning to struggle

Multiple Perspectives on Lab Results

Western Medicine

Focuses on pathology detection—is disease present or not? Values outside range trigger diagnosis and pharmaceutical intervention.

Functional Medicine

Looks for optimal ranges and early dysfunction. Addresses root causes (nutrition, lifestyle, toxins, inflammation, gut health, stress) before pathology develops.

Traditional Chinese Medicine

Correlates lab findings with TCM patterns. Low hemoglobin = Blood deficiency. High uric acid = Damp-Heat. Thyroid issues = Kidney and Spleen disharmony.

Ayurveda

Views lab abnormalities through dosha imbalances. Pitta excess creates inflammatory markers. Vata depletion creates deficiencies. Kapha accumulation creates high cholesterol and sluggish metabolism.

What To Do With Your Results

  1. Request copies: You have the right to your lab results. Keep them.
  2. Track trends: Store all labs together so you can see changes over time.
  3. Ask questions: "Why is this flagged?" "What does this mean functionally?" "What might cause this pattern?"
  4. Consider comprehensive testing: If symptoms persist despite "normal" labs, request more detailed panels.
  5. Explore integrative perspectives: Your values tell different stories through different medical lenses.

When to Dig Deeper

Consider advanced or comprehensive testing if:

  • Symptoms persist despite "normal" standard labs
  • Values are changing (trending up or down) even if still in range
  • Multiple markers are borderline
  • You have strong family history of certain conditions
  • Standard treatment hasn't resolved issues
